# USER_INTERVIEWS.md

These are summaries of 10–15 minute informal conversations conducted with individuals using AI tools in their daily workflows. The goal was to validate assumptions around AI tool spending and redundancy.

---

## Interview 1 — Recent Graduate (Job Seeker)

**Profile:** Friend, recent graduate actively applying for software roles

### Key quotes:
- “I use AI tools almost every day to help me with coding practice and resume building.”
- “I didn’t realize I was paying for multiple tools doing almost the same thing.”
- “Sometimes I feel like I’m spending more on tools than I’m earning right now.”

### Surprising insight:
Even at a low-income stage, AI tools are treated as “necessary infrastructure,” not optional subscriptions.

### Design impact:
This reinforced the need for **clear savings visibility**, especially for individual users, not just teams.

---

## Interview 2 — Software Engineer (Working Professional)

**Profile:** Mid-level software engineer using multiple AI coding assistants

### Key quotes:
- “I switch between ChatGPT, Copilot, and Claude depending on the task.”
- “Honestly I don’t track how much I’m spending across all of them.”
- “There’s definitely overlap, but I’ve never calculated if it’s worth it.”

### Surprising insight:
Tool switching is driven by habit and context, not cost awareness.

### Design impact:
Led to emphasizing **tool overlap detection (redundancy insights)** rather than just total spend breakdown.

---

## Interview 3 — Deloitte Employee (Professional User)

**Profile:** Working professional at Deloitte (consulting environment)

### Key quotes:
- “We use AI tools heavily for writing and summarization tasks.”
- “Costs are usually not tracked at individual level, only at team level.”
- “If someone showed me a breakdown of wasted spend, I’d probably act on it.”

### Surprising insight:
Even in structured enterprise environments, AI tool usage is fragmented and poorly tracked.

### Design impact:
Justified adding **team-level aggregation and optimization suggestions**, not just individual tool analysis.

---

## Interview 4 — Additional Professional (Household/Secondary User Insight)

**Profile:** Spouse of working professional (software/consulting environment exposure)

### Key quotes:
- “I didn’t realize how many different AI subscriptions there are now.”
- “It feels like every task has a different paid tool attached to it.”
- “If there was a way to simplify all of them, I’d definitely use it.”

### Surprising insight:
Non-technical users also feel subscription fatigue from AI tooling ecosystems.

### Design impact:
Strengthened the need for **simple, non-technical explanations in audit summaries.**

---

## Summary

Across all conversations, a consistent pattern emerged:

- Users underestimate total AI spend
- Tool overlap is not intentional but organic
- Cost awareness is extremely low even among engineers
- People are willing to act once inefficiencies are made visible

This validated the core premise of StackSpend:
> Most AI spend waste is invisible, not unknown.
